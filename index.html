Personal Responsibility
and Climate Change

Can we, as individuals, truly influence the global process — or is it just an illusion?

Personal Responsibility for Climate Change

In this article, I would like to assess personal responsibility for climate change. Can we individually affect climate change, or is the process so global that our contribution will never be enough?

In today's industrial‑technological world, people must work to support themselves and their families. Almost every human job in modern reality negatively impacts the ecosystems around us. Many people choose to work in factories and enterprises that are responsible for the main environmental pollution. Our society cannot give up factories, fossil fuel combustion, deforestation, agriculture (especially livestock), industry and waste.

Factories seriously affect the environment through air pollution (CO₂, sulfur, heavy metals), water pollution (wastewater with chemicals), soil pollution (industrial waste, heavy metals) and excessive resource consumption (water, energy), causing acid rain, the greenhouse effect, ecosystem degradation, loss of biodiversity, smog and plastic accumulation. This leads to climate change, health problems for people and animals, and landscape destruction.

The Car — Daily Contribution

To get to work in factories, people use cars. Cars significantly pollute the environment by releasing greenhouse gases (CO₂), nitrogen oxides, sulfur oxides, carbon monoxide and particulate matter, which contributes to climate change and respiratory diseases. They also contaminate soil with heavy metals (lead, cadmium) and petroleum products, and create noise pollution, negatively affecting human health, flora and fauna, and destroying natural habitats.

In cities, road transport accounts for up to 90% of all pollutant emissions. One car can consume more oxygen than a human and emit hundreds of kilograms of harmful substances per year.

Consumption: Meat, Clothing, Transport

The money earned in factories is spent on food (for example, buying meat in a supermarket), maybe on clothes, and ultimately… Livestock production has a negative impact on the environment, causing deforestation for pastures, air pollution with greenhouse gases (methane, ammonia) and PM2.5 particles, soil depletion and erosion, and water pollution with nitrates, phosphates and antibiotics, leading to algal blooms and loss of biodiversity. This industry is one of the largest sources of greenhouse gas emissions, comparable to emissions from all transport.

Clothing production greatly harms the environment, accounting for 10% of global CO₂ emissions, huge consumption of fresh water (up to 2700 litres per t‑shirt) and pollution of water bodies with toxic dyes (20% of industrial wastewater), as well as microplastics (half a million tons per year from synthetics) that enter the ocean and food chains. This is linked to fast fashion, overproduction and waste that takes hundreds of years to decompose.

Food and clothing are now delivered from all over the world using the same types of transport.

Waste — The Final Point

We also leave behind a significant amount of waste… Less than 20% of household waste is recycled worldwide, with 80–90% sent to landfills and 2–3% incinerated. There are strong regional differences, but overall recycling rates are low. For example, only about 9% of plastic is recycled, while leaders like Germany achieve 68% or more — but that is the exception, not the rule.

So we — honest citizens — will of course separate and sort waste, and think about how great we are and how we help nature, but all these efforts are just a drop in the ocean. A psychological indulgence for our society and the individual. The desire to relieve oneself of responsibility using purely ritual actions that actually have no global significance and do not change the big picture.

Can We Fix Anything?

One might object: “So let’s try to fix it!” In factories and plants we will introduce gas cleaning technologies. Install filters, scrubbers, catalysts, modernise processes, optimise equipment (ventilation, sealing) and use safer materials, as well as automation and strict regulations to control and reduce pollution.

In that case, we must understand that these measures will be expensive. The production of a filter itself is a blow to the environment. And they would need to be manufactured in incredible quantities to see any effect from their operation. Plus, we would have to find a way to dispose of old equipment, which cannot always be fully recycled. And also the carbon footprint left by transporting various materials.

As for cars, many people are now replacing petrol cars with electric ones. However, electric car production leaves a large carbon footprint and uses rare‑earth metals. The biggest danger is the batteries, which cannot be fully recycled. Therefore, switching from a conventional car to an electric one solely for environmental protection is not worthwhile. It is better to buy an electric car only if your previous car has worn out.

We could also try to replace plastic with other materials. But alternative materials are more expensive, less reliable, and their production also leaves a carbon footprint. If replacing plastic were economically viable, it would already have been done. Research is ongoing, and attempts are being made to replace plastic with other materials.

As we can see, human activity in the modern industrial‑technological system is continuously linked to environmental pollution. A vicious circle is formed that cannot be broken. Modern society is a consumer society. We take more than we need. We produce huge amounts of waste, knowing that we could avoid such volumes.
Attempts to change something help on a small scale, but globally they will not change anything. If humanity spent money not on wars and arms production, but on timely problem solving, the outcome might be different. We have already passed the point of no return. Now any attempts to change things only buy a little more time until the complete collapse of all ecosystems, rising global temperatures and the extinction of all life. Many consider this perspective exaggerated, but all scientific assessments point to one thing — we are close to catastrophe!

This is perfectly shown in the film “Don't Look Up”, where an asteroid flying toward Earth serves as an allegory for global warming. We stubbornly try not to notice it, to bury our heads in the sand, but that leads to disaster.

So What Should We Do?

In this situation, I think we should start with ourselves. Even if our efforts are globally useless, they can work on a small scale. These efforts will buy us time. When addressing such issues, we can turn to Kant's categorical imperative.

Kant's categorical imperative is the central principle of his ethics — an unconditional moral requirement that prescribes: “Act only according to that maxim whereby you can at the same time will that it should become a universal law.” He also states: “Act in such a way that you treat humanity, whether in your own person or in the person of any other, never merely as a means to an end, but always at the same time as an end.” This principle is an absolute and universal moral law that does not depend on personal desires or consequences, and requires respect for human dignity.

Imagine that everyone in the world acts as you do…

That is why it is so important to take simple actions: reduce electricity use, switch to LED lighting and energy‑efficient appliances. Give up private cars in favour of public transport, cycling or walking where possible. Refuse single‑use plastics, reduce food waste and choose durable goods. Participate in climate initiatives and support politicians who promote the environmental agenda. Sort your waste.
We can also hope for the development of artificial intelligence, which can help develop new strategies and approaches to solving this problem. We must do everything in our power, because there may be hidden parameters — options and opportunities we have overlooked that could later greatly influence the course of events.
And one day, perhaps, the pendulum will swing the other way…
